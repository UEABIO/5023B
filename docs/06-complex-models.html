9.1 Designing a Model

We are introduced to the fruitfly dataset Partridge and Farquhar (1981)1. From our understanding of sexual selection and reproductive biology in fruit flies, we know there is a well established ‘cost’ to reproduction in terms of reduced longevity for female fruitflies. The data from this experiment is designed to test whether increased sexual activity affects the lifespan of male fruitflies.

The flies used were an outbred stock, sexual activity was manipulated by supplying males with either new virgin females each day, previously mated females ( Inseminated, so remating rates are lower), or provide no females at all (Control). All groups were otherwise treated identically.

  • type: type of female companion (virgin, inseminated, control(partners = 0))

  • longevity: lifespan in days

  • thorax: length of thorax in micrometres (a proxy for body size)

  • sleep: percentage of the day spent sleeping

9.2 Hypothesis

Before you start any formal analysis you should think clearly about the sensible parameters to test. In this example, we are most interested in the effect of sexual activity on longevity. But it is possible that other factors may also affect longevity and we should include these in our model as well, and we should think hard about what terms might reasonably be expected to interact with sexual activity to affect longevity.

9.2.1 Independent variables

  • type - should definitely be included.

  • thorax - the size of the flies could determine longevity. Carreira et al (2009)2

  • sleep - sleep could easily help determine longevity. Thompson et al (2020)3

9.2.2 Hypotheses

  • Flies paired with non-virgin females do not live as long, they expend more energy trying to mate with previously mated females.

  • Larger male flies live longer than smaller flies.

  • Flies that sleep more live longer.


lm(longevity ~ type + thorax + sleep, data = dros_clean)

9.2.3 Possible interactions

  • type:sleep - the amount that sleep (rest) helps promote longevity could change depending on how much activity the fly engages in when awake. Chen et al (2017)4

9.2.4 Hypothesis

  • Sleep reduces or offsets the reduction in lifespan experience by males paired with non-virgin females. (rest helps mitigate the stress).

Other interactions could be included but you should have a strong reason for them.

9.5 Designing a model

In this section, we explore how to construct a statistical model to analyze biological data. Specifically, we will build a linear model to examine the effects of mating type, body size, and sleep on longevity in Drosophila melanogaster. We will define our dependent variable (longevity) and include relevant predictor variables (mating type, thorax length, and sleep duration).

Using the lm() function in R, we fit a model to our cleaned dataset, and generate a summary of the results. This approach allows us to assess the significance of each predictor and interpret how these factors influence longevity. By the end of this section, you will understand how to structure a model, choose appropriate variables, and interpret statistical outputs.

# a full model
flyls1 <- lm(longevity ~ type + thorax + sleep, data = dros_clean)

summary(flyls1)

9.6 Model checking & collinearity

Before we start playing with the terms in our model, we should check to see if this is even a good way of fitting and measuring our data. We should check the assumptions of our model are being met.

performance::check_model(flyls1,
                         detrend = F)

  • Posterior Predictive Check – Assesses whether the model’s predicted values align well with the observed data, indicating good model fit.

  • Linearity Check – Ensures that the relationship between predictor variables and the outcome is approximately linear, a key assumption of linear regression.

  • Homogeneity of Variance Check – Tests whether residuals (errors) have constant variance across all levels of predictors, known as homoscedasticity.

  • Influential Observation Check – Identifies individual data points that have a disproportionately large impact on the model’s estimates.

  • Collinearity Check – Detects whether predictor variables are highly correlated with each other, which can distort model estimates and reduce interpretability.

  • Normality Check – Examines whether the residuals of the model follow a normal distribution, an assumption necessary for reliable inference in linear regression.

9.6.4 Model Interpretation

The drop1() function helps assess whether a predictor (e.g. type) significantly improves the model. If removing a variable results in a large decrease in model fit (i.e., a significant drop in the F-statistic), it suggests that the variable is an important contributor to explaining the outcome.

# use drop1 function to remove top-level terms

9.6.4.1 Coefficients and Their Meaning

Each row represents a predictor variable in the model:

  • Intercept (-56.05, t(120) = -5.013, p < 0.001): The expected longevity when all predictor variables are zero (not always biologically meaningful but serves as a reference point).

  • typeInseminated (3.63, t(120) = 1.31, p = 0.193): Males paired with inseminated females lived on average 3.63 days longer than the reference group (control), but this effect is not statistically significant (p > 0.05).

  • typeVirgin (-13.25, t(120) = -4.8, p < 0.001): Males paired with virgin females lived significantly shorter lives, about 13.25 days less than the control group (p < 0.001, highly significant).

  • thorax (144.43, t(120) = 11.01, p < 0.001): Body size (thorax length) has a strong positive effect on longevity—each 1 mm increase in thorax length is associated with an increase of ~144 days in lifespan (p < 0.001).

  • sleep (-0.05, t(120) = -0.83, p = 0.41): Sleep has a very small negative effect on longevity, but this effect is not statistically significant (p > 0.05).

9.6.5 Model Fit Metrics

  • Residual standard error (11.23): Measures how much the observed longevity values deviate from the model’s predictions on average.

  • R-squared (0.6046): About 60% of the variability in longevity is explained by this model, which suggests a reasonably strong fit.

  • F-statistic (45.88, p < 2.2e-16): Indicates that the model overall is statistically significant, meaning at least one predictor contributes significantly to explaining longevity.

9.6.6 Key Takeaways

  • Body size (thorax) has the strongest and most significant effect on longevity.

  • Being paired with virgin females significantly reduces male longevity.

  • Sleep and pairing with inseminated females do not have significant effects.

  • The model explains a substantial portion (60%) of the variation in longevity.

9.7 Reporting Uncertainty

Reporting mean differences along with 95% confidence intervals (CIs) provides several important benefits in reporting:

  • Precision and Uncertainty:

A 95% confidence interval tells you the range within which the true population mean difference likely falls, with 95% confidence. It provides more context than just reporting a single point estimate (the mean difference). This gives the reader a sense of how reliable your estimate is.

  • Statistical Significance:

If the 95% CI for a mean difference does not include zero, it suggests that the difference is statistically significant at the 5% level. This is a quick way to interpret the results without solely relying on P-values.

  • Practical Significance:

The confidence interval gives a sense of the magnitude of the effect and whether it’s large enough to be of practical significance. Even a statistically significant result might not have real-world relevance if the confidence interval is very narrow and the effect size is small.

  • Informed Decision-Making:

Reporting both the point estimate and the confidence interval helps stakeholders, including researchers, practitioners, and policymakers, make better-informed decisions. It reflects both the strength of evidence and its precision.
