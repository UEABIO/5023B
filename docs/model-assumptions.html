11.1 Model assumptions

Linear models are a cornerstone of statistical analysis, widely used across scientific disciplines to explore relationships between variables. Their popularity stems from their simplicity, interpretability, and versatility. However, the reliability of linear models depends on meeting several critical assumptions:

  • Normality of residuals

  • Homogeneity of variance

  • Linearity of relationships

  • Absence of influential outliers

  • Low multicollinearity among predictors.

When these assumptions are violated, the model’s conclusions can become misleading, leading to erroneous inferences and predictions. Diagnosing and addressing these violations is a crucial step in any linear modeling workflow. This chapter explores how to evaluate linear models, identify assumption violations, and apply appropriate corrective measures.

11.2 Missing variables

When learning statistics, it’s important to remember that the first step in diagnosing poor model residuals is to ensure that your model includes the correct predictors and interaction terms. Residuals reflect the difference between the observed and predicted values, and large or patterned residuals often indicate that the model is missing key information.

For example:

If a predictor that strongly affects the outcome is omitted, the model may fail to capture important variation, leading to biased predictions. If an interaction between two predictors (e.g., temperature and humidity) exists but is not included, the model might not adequately describe the relationship between predictors and the outcome.

11.3 Data transformations

When building a linear model, we assume that the residuals (the differences between the observed and predicted values) are small, randomly distributed, and have constant variance. If this isn’t true, the model may not be capturing the relationship between the variables correctly.

Transforming the dependent variable (the variable you’re trying to predict) can help fix these issues by changing its scale to better match the assumptions of the model. Here’s how:

  • Non-normal residuals: If the dependent variable is heavily skewed (e.g., income or population size), transformations like taking the log(y) or square root (y) can make the distribution more symmetric.

  • Stabilize Variance: If the residuals show increasing or decreasing spread (heteroscedasticity), transformations can help make the variance more constant.

  • Linearise Relationships: Some relationships between the dependent and independent variables are non-linear. Transformations can make these relationships more linear, which linear models handle better.

11.3.1 Example

# Model and diagnostics
bacteria_model <- lm(Growth_Rate ~ Temperature, data = bacteria_growth_data)

check_model(bacteria_model,
            detrend = FALSE)

** What are the issues with this model?**

If we look at the residuals we see there are obvious issues with linearity, and heteroscedasticity and normality of the residuals - all of these criteria might be improved with the right data transformation

check_normality(bacteria_model)

check_heteroscedasticity(bacteria_model)
Warning: Non-normality of residuals detected (p < .001).
Warning: Heteroscedasticity (non-constant error variance) detected (p < .001).

11.3.1.1 Boxcox

Box-Cox transformations help identify the best transformation for the dependent variable (y) in a linear model to improve fit, stabilize variance, and address non-normality. The method tests a family of power transformations:

How It Works: The Box-Cox method estimates the optimal value of 𝜆 (the power parameter) that makes the residuals closest to normality and stabilizes variance.

Common transformations include:

  • 𝜆= 1: No transformation (original scale).

  • λ=0: Log transformation.

  • λ=0.5: Square root transformation.

Why Not Use 𝜆 Directly? The optimal 𝜆is often not a simple value like 0, 0.5, or 1. Using a fractional 𝜆(e.g., 0.27) creates a less interpretable transformation. Instead of applying the exact 𝜆 it’s better to choose a nearby, familiar transformation (e.g., log or square root) for ease of interpretation while still improving the model.

11.3.2 Potential drawbacks

Transforming the dependent variable can help fix issues like skewed data or uneven residual patterns, but it can also create new challenges:

  • Interpretability:

After a transformation, the relationship between predictors and the dependent variable becomes harder to interpret. For example, if you log-transform the dependent variable, the model predicts the logarithm of y, not y itself. This makes it less intuitive to explain results on the original scale.

  • Fixing One Issue, Causing Another:

While a transformation might solve one problem (e.g., skewed data), it could create new issues. For instance, a log transformation can stabilize variance but may introduce difficulties if the data contains zero or negative values.

In short, while transformations are useful, they require careful consideration to avoid trading one problem for another or making the results harder to interpret.

11.4 Heteroscedasticity

Heteroscedasticity occurs when the residuals (errors) of a linear model have non-constant variance across the range of the predictors. Even if the data appears normal and linear, heteroscedasticity can still be a problem.

Imagine a situation where:

  • Residuals are larger (more spread out) for higher values of a predictor, or smaller for lower values.

  • Although the relationship between the predictors and the response variable is linear, the variance of the errors changes systematically.

11.4.0.1 Why Does This Matter?

Misleading Inference: Heteroscedasticity doesn’t bias the coefficients of the model, but it affects the calculation of standard errors, leading to incorrect p-values and confidence intervals.

# Model and diagnostics
plant_model <- lm(plant_growth ~ sunlight + water, data = plant_data)

check_model(plant_model,
            detrend = FALSE)
check_heteroscedasticity(plant_model)
check_normality(plant_model)
Warning: Heteroscedasticity (non-constant error variance) detected (p < .001).
OK: residuals appear as normally distributed (p = 0.199).

What are the issues with these model residuals?

11.4.0.2 Transformation

Unlike the previous example we have heteroscedasticity of variance, without non-linearity or non-normal residuals. So what happens when we attempt a dependent variable transformation?

First let’s see what a box-cox transformation suggests?

car::boxCox(plant_model)

Why does the Box-Cox transformation throw an error when the dependent variable contains non-positive values (zero or negative)?

If we add a constant to the dependent variable (enough to make all values positive), the relationship between dependent and independent variables is preserved but the all values are now non-negative.

plant_model <- lm(plant_growth+1 ~ sunlight + water, data = plant_data)

car::boxCox(plant_model)

This indicates a square root transformation could be applied to the model

sqrt_plant_model <- lm(sqrt(plant_growth+1) ~ sunlight + water, data = plant_data)

check_model(sqrt_plant_model,
            detrend = FALSE)
check_heteroscedasticity(sqrt_plant_model)
check_normality(sqrt_plant_model)
Warning: Heteroscedasticity (non-constant error variance) detected (p < .001).
Warning: Non-normality of residuals detected (p = 0.002).

Despite the box-cox predictions, we should be able to see that transforming the dependent variable has actually made this worse.

Not totally unexpected, a data transformation changes the relationship between the predictor variables and the mean/ regression line as well as affecting variance.

11.4.1 Robust errors

When the only issue with a model is heteroscedasticity of variance, the relationship between predictors and the dependent variable can be good, but our standard errors, confidence intervals and p-values will be unreliable.

In a linear model, robust errors are adjustments to the standard errors of the model’s coefficients that make them more reliable when certain assumptions, like constant variance of residuals (homoscedasticity), are violated (giving heteroscedasticity).

Imagine you’re analysing data where some observations have more variability than others. Ordinary linear models assume all residuals have the same variance, so they might underestimate (or overestimate) the uncertainty of your coefficients. Robust errors correct this by accounting for the uneven variance, giving you more reliable p-values and confidence intervals.

In R we can use the package Zeileis & Lumley (2024) to help provide robust errors:

coeftest(plant_model, 
         vcov = vcovHC(plant_model, type = "HC1"))

11.5 Bootstrapping

Bootstrapping is a statistical technique that uses resampling to estimate the variability of a model’s predictions or parameters, such as coefficients and standard errors. Unlike traditional methods that rely on strict assumptions about the data (e.g., normality or constant variance), bootstrapping works by repeatedly sampling from the observed data or residuals to create many simulated datasets we create many versions of what the data “could have looked like.”

This process reflects the natural variability in the data, giving us a realistic measure of uncertainty.

By fitting the model to these resampled datasets, we can calculate how often our estimates and standard errors change - if they are generally constant - we can have confidence they are useful even when they may violate some of the assumptions of the standard linear model.

11.5.1 Things bootstrapping can help with:

  1. Non-Normality of Residuals

    • Problem: Linear models assume that residuals (errors) are normally distributed. If this assumption is violated (e.g., due to skewed data or outliers), the standard errors and confidence intervals based on the normality assumption may be invalid.

    • How Bootstrapping Helps: Bootstrapping does not rely on the residuals being normally distributed. Instead, it estimates variability directly by resampling from the data, providing robust confidence intervals and standard errors even when residuals are non-normal.

  2. Heteroscedasticity (Non-Constant Variance of Residuals)

    • Problem: Linear models assume that residuals have constant variance (homoscedasticity). If the variance of residuals changes across levels of a predictor, standard errors can be underestimated or overestimated.

    • How Bootstrapping Helps: Bootstrapping accounts for heteroscedasticity by resampling residuals with their original variability, providing standard errors and confidence intervals that reflect the true spread of the data.

  3. Small Sample Sizes

    • Problem: Small datasets make it hard to rely on traditional statistical methods, as they often assume large sample sizes for accurate standard errors and confidence intervals.

    • How Bootstrapping Helps: By repeatedly resampling the available data, bootstrapping effectively “amplifies” the sample size, allowing for more stable estimates of variability even with small datasets.

  4. Outliers or Influential Points

    • Problem: Outliers or influential data points can skew the model fit, affecting estimates and residual patterns.

    • How Bootstrapping Helps: Bootstrapping resamples the data, ensuring that results are not overly dependent on specific observations. It gives a more robust estimate of variability that accounts for the influence of outliers.

11.5.2 Limitations of Bootstrapping

While bootstrapping is powerful, it does not address all issues, it cannot help with:

  • Multicollinearity

  • Missing Key Predictors: If an important predictor is omitted, bootstrapping will not fix the resulting bias in the model.

  • Non-linearity: Bootstrapping does not correct for non-linear relationships; re-specifying the model is necessary.

Tip

Bootstrapping can be very powerful, but it doesn’t fix everything (see above).

In addition bootstrapping does not teach us about the real patterns of variance or distribution of residuals in the data. Instead, it works by resampling the observed residuals (or data) and assumes that these resampled patterns are representative of the true variability.

Finally it is computationally intensive as the data is resampled and modelled many times

We can fit bootstrapped parameters using the Lüdecke et al. (2024) package:

  • We usually set the iterations argument to a minimum of 1000 iterations, it may need more. You have to bootstrap until the estimates are stable (appear not to change as iterations increase)

  • If the estimates change only negligibly (e.g., within 1–2%), you have likely run enough iterations.

parameters::bootstrap_parameters(plant_model, iterations = 1000)

11.6 Non-linearity

In linear models, we assume that the relationship between the predictors (independent variables) and the response (dependent variable) is linear—that is, a change in a predictor is associated with a proportional change in the response. However, real-world data often deviate from this assumption, and relationships can be non-linear. For example, the effect of increasing temperature on plant growth might plateau after an optimal range, or the impact of exercise on health might diminish after a certain intensity.

11.6.2 Polynomials

Polynomials are mathematical expressions that allow us to model non-linear relationships between predictors and the response variable in a linear model. By including polynomial terms (e.g., x^2, x^3), we can create more flexible models to capture curvature in the data.

11.6.2.1 What Does poly(x, 2) Do?

  • poly(x, 2) creates a second-degree polynomial (quadratic).
  • It models the relationship as: [ y = _0 + _1 x + _2 x^2 + ]
  • This adds curvature to the model, allowing it to capture a U-shape or an inverted U-shape.
11.6.2.1.1 Example:

If the data looks like a parabola (e.g., plant growth increasing with temperature up to a point, then declining), a second-degree polynomial can model this.

11.6.2.2 What Does poly(x, 3) Do?

  • poly(x, 3) creates a third-degree polynomial (cubic).

  • It models the relationship as: [ y = _0 + _1 x + _2 x^2 + _3 x^3 + ]

  • This allows for more flexibility in the curve, capturing an S-shaped pattern or two bends in the data.

11.6.2.2.1 Example:

If the relationship involves multiple changes in direction (e.g., population growth speeding up, then slowing down, and finally stabilizing), a cubic polynomial can model this.

11.6.2.3 Janka data

In our example the slight curvature in the model could be addressed with a quadratic term:

janka_poly<- lm(hardness ~ poly(dens,2), data = janka)

But we can see this does not address the issues of heteroscedasticity or non-normality of residuals.

performance::check_model(janka_poly)

We know that the log transformed dependent variable did address this, but introduced unwarranted curvature - we could address this with a polynomial:

janka_polylog<- lm(log(hardness) ~ poly(dens,2), data = janka)
performance::check_model(janka_polylog)

11.6.2.4 Polynomial summary

Polynomials can help improve the fit of a model when there is a non-linear relationship. When you do this focus on the overall model fit and visualizations, as the coefficients from poly() are not directly tied to raw powers of x. Because of the lack of coefficient interpretation use this with caution

  • poly(x, 2) (Quadratic): Adds curvature, useful for simple bends like U-shapes.

  • poly(x, 3) (Cubic): Adds more flexibility, useful for S-shaped or complex curves.

In our advanced modules we will look at Generalised Linear Models which retain the benefits of modelling non-linear relationships with better interpretability

11.7 Multicollinearity

Multicollinearity occurs when predictor variables in a linear model are highly correlated, which can make it hard to isolate the effect of individual predictors.

If the primary aim of our model is prediction we do not need to be concerned about multicollinearity as our model will predict new data well

If our aim is to identify significant predictors then multicollinearity makes it difficult to produce accurate estimates and standard errors:

However, not all types of multicollinearity need to be a concern.

11.7.1 Structural Multicollinearity

  • What it is: This happens when we create new variables (e.g., interaction terms or quadratic terms) that are mathematically related to the original variable naturally introduces multicollinearity

  • Do we need to worry?: No, structural multicollinearity is expected and doesn’t affect the validity of the model. It can often be reduced by mean-centering or standardising the variables, but this is often unnecessary.

11.7.2 Predictor Multicollinearity

  • What it is: This occurs when two or more predictors are strongly correlated in the dataset (e.g., temperature and humidity in an environmental study). This can inflate standard errors and make it difficult to interpret individual effects.

  • Do we need to worry?: Sometimes. If your goal is prediction, multicollinearity is less of an issue because the model’s overall performance is what matters. However, if you’re interested in understanding the effect of individual predictors, you may need to address it.

Predictor Multicollinearity can be addressed using simple techniques like variable selection, where we selectively remove terms from the model, this can address inflated standard errors, but requires careful thought over removing terms. More advanced technqiues include principal components analysis (PCA), or regularization (e.g., ridge regression).

The Mammals dataset includes information about various mammal species, focusing on their physical characteristics and ecoogicial traits.

11.7.5 Correcting for multicollinearity

In this instance we have no structural multicollinearity and must conclude there is genuine multicollinearity between our measured variables.

There are advanced techniques for dealing with predictor multicollinearity, but the simplest method is to identify the variables causing mulitcollinearity and use your biological expertise to determine which variables can be removed.

In this instance I should remove one of predation/danger and one of brain_wt/body_wt.

I have decided that overall danger and brain_wt are the most important variables so will retain these:

mammal_model2 <- lm(total_sleep ~ life_span + gestation + brain_wt +
                   danger + exposure, data=mammals)

performance::check_model(mammal_model2)

I can see that my multicollinearity has gone AND my model is still a good fit.

Importantly if I compare the summaries of my old and new models, I can see that my adjusted R squared has only dropped slightly and that gestation period now appears to be a significant predictor of sleep duration - previously this relationship was masked!

11.8 Outliers

Outliers have been left to last because they should be dealt with last. Outliers can be the result of a poor model fit, non-linearity or non-normal residuals. As such they may be resolved when attempting to fix other issues with a model.

Cope’s rule refers to the tendency of animal body sizes to increase in time with increasing age of the lineage. De Souza and Santucci (2014) tested this using a set of data from the dinosaur clade called the Titanosaurs. This is a group of sauropods that included some of the largest animals known in the history of the Earth (e.g. Argentinosaurus) and which were widespread and diverse during the Cretaceous period. Most Titanosaurs are known from only a few bones, and the massive limb bones are particularly common: In this dataset we wish to see whether there is a significant relationship between two sets of bones, so that if we only have access to the Humerus we can predict the size of the Femur and the overall size of the dinsosaur:

titanosaur_model <- lm(Femur ~ Humerus, data = titanosaurs)

11.9 Advanced Data Analysis

Linear models are a foundational tool in data analysis, providing a straightforward way to understand relationships between variables. However, they are just the beginning. As you progress, more advanced techniques will equip you to tackle a wider range of data and research questions:

  • Generalized Linear Models (GLMs): Extend linear models to handle different types of response variables, such as counts (Poisson regression) or proportions (logistic regression).

  • Mixed Models: Account for hierarchical or grouped data, such as repeated measures or experiments with nested designs, by including both fixed and random effects.

  • Machine Learning Algorithms: Focus on predictive accuracy and uncovering complex patterns in data using techniques like decision trees, random forests, and neural networks.

  • Classification: Specialized methods, like logistic regression or support vector machines, are used to predict categorical outcomes (e.g., disease presence or absence).

  • Principal Component Analysis (PCA): A dimensionality reduction technique that simplifies large datasets by transforming variables into a smaller set of uncorrelated components while retaining most of the variability.

Each of these methods builds on the principles of linear models, expanding your ability to analyse complex data, make predictions, and extract meaningful insights across diverse contexts. Your journey in data analysis has only just begun!
